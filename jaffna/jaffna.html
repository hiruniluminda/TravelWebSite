WELCOME TO JAFFNA DISTRICT

Jaffna District is a district in the Northern Province of Sri Lanka. The total land area of the district is 1,025 square kilometers and the total population is 583,378. The main city of this district is Jaffna.
We welcome you to find your unique Jaffna district travel experience.

Sangilean Thoopu

It is an ancient faade and one of the ruins of the Kingdom of Jaffna. Cankillian Thoppu is an Archaeological Preservation Monument in the Jaffna District. The architecture here is typical of Dutch colonial architecture, but is believed to be part of the facade of the king's palace.

Nallur Temple

Nallur Kandaswamy Kovil is a unique Hindu temple located in the Jaffna District of the Northern Province of Sri Lanka. It is located in Nallur. The main deity of the temple is Lord Murugan.

Jaffna Fort

Jaffna Fort is a historic fort located in the Jaffna District of Sri Lanka. It was also built by the Portuguese and the Dutch.

Dambakola Patuna

Dambakola Patuna is an ancient port on the land now owned by Sri Lanka. According to historical sources, Ven. Sangamitta Thera came to this place with the Sri Maha Bodhi.

Jaffna Library

Built and completed in 1933 under British rule. It is said to be one of the largest libraries in Asia with over 97,000 books and manuscripts in the early 1980s. However, as a result of the unrest in Sri Lanka during that period, a group of extremists set fire to the Jaffna Library in 1981, destroying many of the library's valuable books. Renovation work on the destroyed library was completed in 2001.

Delft Island

The island is a land of ruins from several eras. There is an ancient dagoba in one place and the Bob tree on the island has a hole large enough for two people to crawl into. Ancient snake statues and Hindu temples offered by Hindu devotees are located on the island. Horse stables and forts that also housed horses during the colonial period still remain on the island in ruins. There are also ruins, including an old hospital building with a pigeon storage tower. At Delft, a footprint three to four times larger than the average human foot can be seen on the limestone slab that is considered to be Adam's footprint.
